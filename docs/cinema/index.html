The Cinema Sessions

Brut's most ambitious workflow: turn a handful of stills into a finished, cut sequence — planned by a crew of AI agents, shot on the video provider of your choice, and assembled locally with ffmpeg, for exactly the bill it showed you.

Stills → a crew that plans the film → a storyboard you edit → clips shot behind a confirm gate → a sequence cut on your machine. You stay in control at every step.

#The pipeline

Conform → Reference Board → Scenarist → Artistic Director → DoP → Script Supervisor
        → Storyboard → Film Shots → Clip Board → Sequence → Preview

Every arrow is a wire. The agents pass a plan document (JSON on text wires); the boards are where you review and curate.

Don't build this by hand the first time: the whole chain ships as the Cinema Crew template — File ▾ → Browse library… → Cinema Crew. It arrives pre-wired with sensible defaults (invented-first reel brief, cheap planning models, keyframes on Nano Banana); you add stills, pick your video provider, and run. Let's walk what it does.

#1 · Prepare the stills

Start from your renders — an Image In node (or a Multi-Shot contact sheet you've curated). Run them through Conform to a single aspect ratio (video wants consistency), cropping each to keep the right part of the frame.

Wire the conformed stills into a Reference Board. This makes the pool visible and numbered — and the tile number is the still number the crew will refer to. Reorder to taste; the numbers are the contract.

#2 · The crew plans the film

Chain the four agents, each grounded on the stills:

  • Scenarist writes the treatment (one shot = one still — or an invented shot, below).
  • Artistic Director sets the look — palette, light, mood.
  • DoP turns it into an executable shot plan: camera, motion, duration, and which still grounds each clip.
  • Script Supervisor harmonizes speed, light and palette across the whole thing.

Pick the LLM for each — Gemini, GPT or Claude — on your own key. Each plan is editable text: tweak a line and it flows downstream; the analysis shows in the Activity panel.

#Invented shots — the flourish

Your stills define the project's identity, not the shot list. With a brief like Cinematic Reel (flourish), the Scenarist may also invent compositions no still contains — a detail close-up, a reveal, subtle human presence, the pull-back ending — each anchored on the stills that carry its materials, with a written keyframe prompt. The Artistic Director styles those prompts along with everything else.

The rule never loosens, it moves one level: one clip = one keyframe. An invented shot's start frame is generated (image-to-image on its anchor stills, with the identity clause appended, conformed to the film's aspect) before any video runs. An invented shot is never quietly sent as text-to-video — without an approved keyframe it simply refuses to roll.

Want the full reel behavior? The Scenarist's "Invent every shot" switch makes the stills references only — no shot animates one directly, every start frame is generated. Off, the brief decides shot by shot.

#3 · The storyboard — your edit bay

The Storyboard turns the plan into shot cards: per-clip start/end frame swaps with thumbnails, editable and lockable prompts, add/reorder/remove shots. Change a line here and only that shot re-renders. It emits concrete image paths, so the next node just needs the plan wire.

Invented shots arrive frameless. Each shows its keyframe prompt and a 🎨 Generate keyframe button — pick the image model, resolution and (on GPT Image) quality once at the top of the board (the pending count and per-frame price sit next to it and follow your picks; keyframes are cents, clips are dollars). Generated keyframes cache on their prompt + anchors (an unchanged card never re-pays), ↻ re-rolls a fresh take, 📌 pins one against later prompt edits, and wiring any image into the card's socket overrides everything. Prefer to ground an invented shot on a real still after all? Just pick one in its start selector.

The film's aspect is your call. The board's aspect dropdown (default: whatever the DoP wrote) conforms every keyframe to the format you choose and stamps it into the emitted plan. Switching aspect later doesn't re-bill: the raw render is kept and simply re-cropped, free.

Batch it. ▶ on the Storyboard (or the 🎨 Generate N button) renders every pending keyframe behind one itemized confirmation — shot list, per-frame price, total. The per-card 🎨 stays for surgical re-work. Declining isn't an error: the plan flows on and Film Shots simply blocks the frameless clips.

Look at your frames. Click any card's image to magnify it full-screen, then browse the whole board like a gallery with ← / → (arrows or the on-screen ‹ ›); Esc closes. And every card has an output plug on its right edge carrying that clip's resolved start frame — the generated keyframe, the wired image, or the grounded still — so you can route a frame anywhere: into an Upscale Image for a delivery-res still, a Compose, a Preview. A card whose keyframe is still pending delivers nothing (never a wrong frame).

#4 · Shoot — behind the confirm gate

Wire the Storyboard into Film Shots. It prices every clip — model, duration, resolution, cost — and shows an itemized confirmation. Nothing generates until you approve the total; cancel a line and it updates. Cached and pinned clips cost $0. Then it fans out, staggered to respect rate limits, with per-clip re-roll / ✓✗ / pin.

#5 · Review & cut

The Clip Board plays every clip — keep the good, ✗ the misses, ↻ re-roll one. Route the keepers into a Sequence node, which assembles them into one mp4 with local ffmpeg — cuts or crossfades, free. Drop a Preview on the end.

Want it in 4K? Route a hero clip (or the assembled film) through Upscale Video — Topaz Proteus or Gaia CG, audio kept, itemized confirm before any spend. The shipped Cinema Crew template already ends with this finish-in-4K tail (provider unset — the money choice stays yours).

#Make it reusable

Select the four agents and collapse them into a "Cinema Team" composite — one droppable node with the stills as its inlets and the plan as its outlet. Save it to the library and start every film from it.

Why it holds together: grounding is enforced in code, not prose — one clip to one keyframe (a still of yours, or a frame generated from your stills), the DoP's still indices reconciled automatically, invented shots blocked until their keyframe exists, an anti-shake stability clause on every clip. The plan you approve is the film you get.
